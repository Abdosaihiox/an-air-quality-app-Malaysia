causes of pollution in malaysia

Transboundary Haze

This is a recurring issue in Malaysia, particularly during the dry season between late June and September. Haze occurs when smoke from agricultural fires in neighboring Indonesia travels across the border, causing air quality to plummet. These fires are often deliberately set to clear land for farming, and the resulting haze can have serious health consequences for Malaysians.

Impact of Vehicle Emissions

Traffic congestion is a major problem in Malaysian cities, and vehicle emissions are a significant contributor to air pollution. The government is taking steps to address this issue, such as promoting the use of public transportation and electric vehicles.

Industrial Air Pollution

Industrial facilities can release a variety of pollutants into the air, including particulate matter, nitrogen oxides, and sulfur oxides. These pollutants can cause respiratory problems, heart disease, and other health problems. The Department of Environment (DOE) is responsible for monitoring and regulating industrial air emissions in Malaysia

how to protect our planet?

Protecting nature requires a collective effort. Individuals can adopt sustainable practices like reducing waste, consuming responsibly, and supporting eco-friendly businesses. Governments can implement stricter environmental regulations, invest in renewable energy, and promote sustainable practices in agriculture and infrastructure. Technological advancements like satellite monitoring and eco-tourism can aid conservation efforts. Finally, market-based solutions such as carbon pricing and Payment for Ecosystem Services can incentivize responsible behavior. By working together through these various approaches, we can ensure a healthier planet for all.

Air Quality in Malaysia

US AQI

The U.S. AQI is EPA's index for reporting air quality. Think of the AQI as a yardstick that runs from 0 to 500. The higher the AQI value, the greater the level of air pollution and the greater the health concern. For example, an AQI value of 50 or below represents good air quality, while an AQI value over 300 represents hazardous air quality.

Good 0-50
Moderate 51-100
Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups 101-150
Unhealthy 151-200
Very Unhealthy 201-300
Hazardous 301+

PM2.5

Fine particulate matter (PM2.5) is an air pollutant that is a concern for people's health when levels in air are high. PM2.5 are tiny particles in the air that reduce visibility and cause the air to appear hazy when levels are elevated.
